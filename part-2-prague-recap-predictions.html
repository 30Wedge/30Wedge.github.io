[Part 2] -- Prague Recap - Predictions

Posted on Fri 23 June 2017 in Prague Study Abroad

Here's me responding to the speculations I made at the beginning of the semester

Will lecture attendance be mandatory?

Not a chance.

Spot on.

My Economics professor was very straightforward about lectures being completely optional. On more than one occasion, I've been the only one who goes to a lecture. On more than one occasion, I've skipped a lecture. One time, one of my friends was 45 minutes late to a 2 hour lecture for no good reason. In retrospect, I probably could have skipped one or two of the less useful lectures more often.

Will the textbooks be cheaper?

Yes, but I'm also guessing that piracy will be more common.

I actually missed my window to buy the English version of recommended textbooks from the book store, so I can't give a good answer.

That being said, none of the instructors required a textbook and only one or two listed one as recommended. I didn't really hear anyone talking about pirating textbooks this semester. I can't tell if that's because it wasn't a big enough deal to talk about, or nobody studying abroad needs textbooks in the first place

To be fair, I only talked to foreign students about this, and only a few times.

One German guy told me that his most expensive textbook was something close to $55, which I thought was funny.

Will final exams revolve around rote memorization or concepts?

Tough one, but I think that finals will be mostly conceptual.

There was a good mix of both.

The final for Microcontrollers was 11 or 12 questions (one for each lecture) asking for basically a summary of that lecture -- kind of bland rote learning imho, but at least the lectures involved some cool hands on examples with one of those cool PIC development boards.

At the final for Applied Electronics, our professor brought us down to the computer lab and had us design a PCB from drawing the schematic, to drawing component footprints to routing traces through the PCB. This was anything but rote.

My Computer Architecture class will have a verbal component to the final.

The verbal component turned out to be a second chance on questions you goofed up. After submitting my exam, the professor called me over to his desk to ask a few questions that started with "Are you sure that you meant to..."

I appreciated this part of the exam, because I completely misinterpreted one of the questions and was able to prove that I knew it verbally. I don't think this system would scale well to classes larger than 8 people.

I'd be pretty pissed if that means I have to memorize the MIPS instruction set to regurgitate on demand.

I wasn't required to memorize it, but I benifited a lot from remembering a good deal of it. It seemed like a reasonable ammount of detail to expect.

Will there be a greater stress on student independence than in the U.S.?

Absolutely.

Nailed it.

I felt so much more independent than in class at UML.

The mentality here is that it's pretty much on your responsibility to learn what you have to. Professors are helpful (mostly) when you need to ask a question, but there was very little hand-holding going on during the parts where I actually learned. This goes along with the fact that lectures were optional.

Will classes overall be easier or harder than UMass Lowell?

I think the mean class here will be more difficult than UML.

Nahhh.

I think that the philosophy of Erasmus classes puts formal education temporarily behind multicultural exchange. Don't get me wrong, I learned a sh*t ton from Computer Architectures and Operating Systems (my two toughest classes), but the workload was less demanding than at UML.

Mostly because the courses I am taking are just more difficult subjects, and partially because some of the Erasmus students hadn't really heard the term "Easy-A." I don't think that joke classes are a "thing" here.

The European term for an "Easy-A" is an "Erasmus course." Maybe not an Easy-A, but an Easy-Pass, because most professors will make it very clear how much work you need to do pass and will remind you how many points you need to get on the final for a 'C' if you ask.

So maybe an "Easy-A" isn't the same as "Erasmus-easy." Instead of participation credit, I would have just one moderately difficult exam that counted for the majority of my grade and multiple chances to retake it.

Here's what happened to the priorities I outlined at the beginning

I've got a list of priorities for the way I spend my time that I plan to stick to:

  1. Travel
  2. Sleep
  3. Academics
  4. Social
  5. Exercise
  6. Down time

The semester ended with the following:

  1. Social
  2. Travel
  3. Sleep
  4. Academics
  5. Exercise
  6. Down time

Basically social interactions jumped from the 4-spot on the list to the top. I felt like the most interesting parts about this semester came from some of the people I met, from a meme-lord from Kansas to a graduate student from Syria.

I think I traveled somewhere five out of the first six weekends, but after just a few weekends in Prague hanging out with the other exchange students, I started to value that interaction a whole lot more.

Excercise-wise, I did enough to feel good. I ran regularly until tweaking my knee during the Prague Half Marathon, and haven't really gone on a long run since. I might have put on 10 pounds, but I don't regret it.

I want to travel every single weekend and milk the life out of the nights. This might be my only time ever in Europe, and I don't want to feel like I missed out on anything.

That was fun for the first month or so, but that kind of travel schedule takes a toll on you for a while. After a while, I didn't want to travel every weekend, but I definitely kept milking the life out of this semester. Like I said, some of the best days I had were just sitting in the parks of Prague with friends and doing nothing.

And I will come back to Europe for sure. Hopefully I'll see some of the friends I met another day.

I don't feel like I missed out on anything.

My pseudo-imperative rules pretty much got shredded along the way.

Asside from that I'll keep the same general personal rules that I do at UML.

  • Never skip class unless something MUCH better comes up.

I slept in once or twice through an early class and maybe dropped down to 50% attendance on a few lectures I really didn't like. This part turned out to be a pretty minor deal, because attendance was anything but mandatory and I could study more effectively on my own.

  • Do homework as soon as possible and study some every weekday.

I wound up studying a little every day, but I also found myself pushing projects off until a little too late for comfort. There were better things to do, and in the end I passed my classes.

  • 8 hours of sleep should be normal every night.

Sleep was a big deal for me before coming over. I think I'm averaging about 6:30h / night now at the end of the semester. There's a lot of factors working against good sleep: international travel is much cheaper at odd times of day, night life doesn't really get going until 11:30pm, and my brain seemed pretty much hardwired to wake up at 8:00am at the latest.

Anyways, I felt like this semester lived up to my expectations while still surprising me.
