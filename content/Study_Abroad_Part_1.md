Title: [Part 1] -- Expectations of Electrical Engineering Classes in Prague
Date: Thu 16 February 2017
Category: Prague Study Abroad

I'm an American exchange student studying Electrical Engineering at Czech Technical Institute in Prague with 200+ other international students for the Spring semester of 2017. 
This article covers my first impressions and expectations one week after arrival. Part 2 will compare my expectations with my experience and then identify the differences between EE courses in the USA.


I'm limiting this series to only two posts so I'll stay motivated to actually follow up


##What's Happened So Far in One Week?
Not a whole lot (academically speaking). 


I arrived, moved in to my dorm, registered for courses and toured the buildings. 
Most of the assistance I've received has been from the International Student Club (ISC) as opposed to staff, which impressed me.


The faculty here seems to be dependent on paper forms (which did _not_ impress me). 
I've had to _physically_ cut and paste a picture of myself onto 5 different documents.
 Moving into the dorm involved paying first month's rent in cash -- $175 (less than a third than a modest Lowell appartment).
Financially, life is good for a U.S. exchange student.


What I can't really judge yet is the classes themselves, but I'll go out on a limb and extrapolate a bit.


##The Students at CTU
I can't say enough good things about the Czech students at CTU that I've met so far.
Every interaction has been positive. 
A student picked me up from the airport and another brought me to the metro office and helped me buy a public-transportation pass; another helped me get my student ID. 
 Then socially, I met a handful of other Czech students at the pub that were really pretty friendly.


They tell me that a huge ammount of young people in Prague speak English, but fewer of the older and rural people speak English.
I guess this is the case with quite a bit of Europe.
 Of the 200+ exchange students, only about 25 are American.
 The rest are from all over.
 Most people that I talk to seem more interested in American music, movies and politics than I am.
 Which is cool.


Anyways, Czech is a hard language to learn.
 Most of the international students don't seem interested in learning Czech.
 Most of the bars/clubs have menus in English anyways, and respond to me in English when I try to open with "pivo prosim."


The student clubs at CTU have been noticably different from the clubs at home.
 For example, there is a student club that maintains the facilities in my dormotory.
 I can join for $20 and get access to the wired internet, gym room and printing service that they maintain.


The ISC is something else too.
 They've put together a handful of "school-sponsored parties" already.
 Whenever I hear school-sponsored, I usually recoil a little bit, because that usually means sub-par catering, a powerpoint presentation and an attendance of 12 +/- 5. 
But no, "school sponsored" here means that the ISC reserves an _entire_ bar or club and then hundreds of international students show up to drink beer together.
 Its quite a bit more in line with what students actually want to do.


Part of the difficulty of the U.S. "School sponsored party" is the drinking age in the states. That's something that the Europeans I've met like to critique.
 I only turned 21 the week of my flight to the Czech Republic.
 I've never been to an American bar.
 But man, the Prague bars are a really good time.
 Beer is cheaper than bottled water too, so, financially, its pretty reasonable to go have a beer after class every day (by the way, my dorm has a bar in the basement and a pub on the same block).


The Czech Republic has an enormous beer culture. 
You would be very hard pressed to go without drinking a beer anywhere you go.
Every social interaction seems to revolve around beer (I went to a rock climbing gym that had a bar built-in).
There isn't a heavy binge drinking culture though that I've noticed.
 Usually the only people I've seen order hard liquor at the bar have been the international students.


Back to academics. I had to digress about beer because it is such an integral part of the culture here.
There's a lot of evidence of some very motivated students.
I've seen 4 different beautiful SAE student-made race cars on the CTU campus, one might have been electric too.
I'm talking really _beautiful_ cars. Cars with three spoilers with flawless molded fiberglass bodies with stripes over glossy black paint (instead of exposed painted JV-welded frames that seem to be more common).
I even saw what looked like a piece of a student-built single-person jet in a hallway.
All along the hallways were display cases filled with examples of student projects that were actually _beautiful_.
Not just functional but aesthetic.
When I get back, I'll be a little more motivated to step up my capstone project to bring some of that artistic talent back to the Ball 4th floor hallway.
I would really like to meet someone from their design teams to try to figure out what makes their culture so successful.


##The Faculty at CTU
I've only had a few interactions. The few prof's I met seemed to be relatively distant with no rush to be anywhere.
The word _aloof_ fits well.


We heard an introductory speech from a handful of the professors so far.
 They talked slowly, but with very good English (If I had to judge, I'd guess that the median professor here has better english than the median professor at home).
They did some very "professorly" things -- like post important URLs on powerpoints that were not electronically available to us (the kind of thing you see and then try to scribble down before they advance the slide).


Things happen less efficiently here than in the states.
 It took 20 minutes at a window to get my public transportation pass.
 Our welcome day speech from the professors included two 30-minute coffee breaks (the speaches themselves probably took 3-4 hours).


Generally speaking, things seem less efficient here. 
At home, I like having one single student card attached to one central student account that I can use to pay for lunch, access my class buildings, get into the gym etc.
Here, I have a seperate paper form keeping track of my student status, international student status, public transit, course record and rent payment.
There's also two online accounts -- one for CUT and a seperate one for the Faculty of Electrical Engineering.
If I want to change my thumbnail picture for either accounts, I can't just upload a picture.
 I have to visit _another_ office.


Those are really just trivial complaints so far. I'm still culture-shocked
 I appreciate that the school here keeps spending practical.
 I haven't seen any stupid $90M buildings here that don't have any classrooms.


I haven't seen the labs yet.
 I signed a generic lab-safety form that looked exactly like the ones in the US.
 I don't think these will differ much.


I also haven't seen any of the classrooms I'll have. It was a little surprising to me that there wasn't some kind of tour around the Electrical Engineering buildings, but thats ok. I'll be happier figuring it out on my own anyways.


##Learning Philosophy
###Erasmus?
Erasmus is a word I learned from the European students.
 It is a program where your government _pays_ you to study abroad in another European country.
 The majority of the students here are "on Erasmus."


From what I've heard about Erasmus, is that the classes are significantly easier and the students focus is about stretching your savings to cover as much traveling and drinking as possible.


That's mostly okay with me, but at the same time I'm trying to take some of the core heavy-lifting courses for my major. 
I don't want to skimp out on the learning.


I'm moving forwards with a lot of energy and a balanced mindset.


###Predictions
I'm predicting the following things about the upcoming semester:


Will lecture attendance be mandatory?

>Not a chance.


Will the textbooks be cheaper?


>Yes, but I'm also guessing that piracy will be more common.


Will final exams revolve around rote memorization or concepts?

>Tough one, but I think that finals will be mostly conceptual.
>My Computer Architecture class will have a verbal component to the final.
>I'd be pretty pissed if that means I have to memorize the MIPS instruction set to regurgitate on demand.


Will there be a greater stress on student independence than in the U.S.?

>Absolutely.

Will classes **overall** be easier or harder than UMass Lowell?

>I think the mean class here will be more difficult than UML.
>Mostly because the courses I am taking are just more difficult subjects, and partially because some of the Erasmus students hadn't really heard the >term "Easy-A." I don't think that joke classes are a "thing" here. 


###The Strategy
I've got a list of priorities for the way I spend my time that I plan to stick to:

 - Travel
 - Sleep
 - Academics
 - Social
 - Exercise
 - Down time

I want to travel every single weekend and milk the life out of the nights. This might be my only time ever in Europe, and I don't want to feel like I missed out on anything.


Asside from that I'll keep the same general personal rules that I do at UML.
 - Never skip class unless something MUCH better comes up.
 - Do homework as soon as possible and study some every weekday.
 - 8 hours of sleep should be normal every night.
